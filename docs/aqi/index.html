Single home for a suite of water quality products

Strategic SSO launch to support acquisition and growth business model.

Team

UX designers, agile development team, product leadership.

Role

Product designer

Tools

Stakeholder interviews, MVP & target mapping, user research, product design

Duration

9 weeks

Business case

Driven by acquisition and growth, after a number of new products joined water quality portfolio, leadership suggested investigate possible solutions to resolve poor cross-app workflows.

1

Multiple products are acquired.

2

Current customers are upsold into more software

3

More users start using multiple product families at once.

4

End users are unsatisfied with cross-app workflows, support is overloaded.

Problem statement

How might we allow water quality specialists acess a portfolio of projects and services in a single touch?

Landing on a persona

Though series of user interviews a target user was defined.

Rosa, manager at multiple water facilities

“I just want my day to run smoothly, no accidents, no sudden expences and budget overruns. happy staff and happy people in the city drinking clear water.”

Pain points

Budget overrund, staff safety, software crashes, low technology saviness

Goal

Have facilities run smoothly, be able to monitor activities though the software.

Qualitative data collection accuracy

After conducting 6 user interviews with user interviews, we had 90% accuracy in customer feedback and high certainty in next steps.

User pain points

No time to trial new software

Trying out new software requires a lot of setup and learning. End users are not willing to spend their time on no-priority work.

Lacking visual and delightful content

All the applications did not look unified, thus similar workflows required different steps to perform.

Poor connection with customer support

Current customer support system was not acessible to low permission end users, so many bugs and requests would not get resolved for ages.

Iterating on design

Sign in

Before: no band identity

At the beginning sign in page looked basic and told very little story about the brand.

After: breathable space

Improved layout grows recognition of the brand.

Home

Before: “not an eye candy” - user

Although the page clearly communicated its purpose, none of the users showed excitement towards the tool.

After: launcher takes up space

Revised visual information architechture prioratizes main actions and gives space for the app links to breathe.

Discover

Before: marketing that's not appliackable to many

Discover space felt like a store where you cannot buy anything because none of the end users have the authority to purchase software.

After: use cases shown only to accountable users

FMarketing would be able to take advantage of avaliable space and generate content for admin users.

Cross-app navigation

Before: conflicting visual hierarchy

Two of the products already have internal app-switcher. Thus app home link had to be integrated within native switcher in the apps. And because each app is different is is incredibly hard to achieve perfect visual hierarchy and consistency balance.

After: project-based notifications

Instead of linking people to the apps, the ideas was to link people to the projects within the app by using notification styling.

Success metrics

A digital touchpoint connects all water quality products and services including training and support

Target users reportedly have control over all of their applications and services from one place.

Increased cross app collaboration across major user groups

Google analytics reported increased activity during workday hours in less popular application features that’s connected to discovery of new workflows.

Reduced tech debt

In anticipation to have more products join the water quality family, it is expected that the merging transition would take less time with refracted code.

Lessons learned

Importance of advocating for a user

Being one of 4 designers in a company with over 70 developers has taught me how important it is to amplify voices of our current and future customers. When entire company says “no, we do not have budget/time/interest/not our focus group”, it has been up to me to frame an argument to address user needs and influence to build a successful product.

Learning business and developer languages: translating user needs into business value

Each stakeholder has a different perspective on UX and the role of UX in products. More importantly, everyone had different levels of empathy towards the user. After doing multiple user testing, and inviting stakeholders to them, growing design maturity internally was key stategy to integrsting user feedback into product design process.
